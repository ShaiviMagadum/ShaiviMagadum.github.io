Unit 1 — Data Concepts

Home Unit 1: Data Unit 2: Networks Politics Interactive Game

Key Vocabulary (Unit 1)

  • Bit: A binary digit (0 or 1).
  • Byte: 8 bits — a common unit for storing data.
  • Roundoff: Error that appears when numbers are approximated due to limited bits.
  • Analog Data: Smooth continuous values (e.g., sound waves).
  • Lossless: Compression that preserves every bit so the original can be recovered exactly.
  • Lossy: Compression that discards some information for smaller file sizes (e.g., JPEG, MP3).
  • Metadata: Data about data — file properties, timestamps, tags.

Interactive: Binary (0–255) → 8-bit representation

Type a number between 0 and 255 to see its 8-bit byte representation and how bits map to bytes.

Byte: 8 bits. Each bit represents a power of two (128→1).

Analog → Digital

Analog signals are continuous; digital signals sample and quantize values into bits and bytes. This is how music, images, and video are transmitted across the internet.

Lossless vs Lossy

Lossless formats (like PNG or FLAC) keep every bit so original data can be restored. Lossy formats (JPEG, MP3) discard parts of the data to reduce size — useful for bandwidth-limited scenarios but not ideal for archival.

Unit 2 — Computer Systems & Networks

Home Unit 1: Data Unit 2: Networks Politics Interactive Game

Key Vocabulary (Unit 2)

  • Computing Device: e.g., phone, server, sensor.
  • Computer Network: interconnected devices exchanging data.
  • Path: the series of connections packets travel on.
  • Protocol: rules systems follow to communicate.
  • Fault Tolerant: continues to operate despite failures.
  • Scalability: ability to grow to handle more devices.
  • Bandwidth: bits per second; capacity of a path.
  • Packets: small chunks of messages that travel independently.
  • Routers: devices that forward packets across networks.
  • DNS: maps URLs to IP addresses.

Interactive: Packet Path Animator

Click "Find path" to compute a route between two devices and watch a packet travel along that path. This demonstrates how routers forward packets along paths chosen by routing protocols.

Internet Structure & Layers

The internet is layered: physical connections, IP routing, transport (TCP/UDP), and application-level protocols (HTTP). Redundancy, fault tolerance, and routing algorithms allow packets to take alternate paths if part of the network fails.

Political Ramifications of Net Neutrality

Home Unit 1: Data Unit 2: Networks Politics Interactive Game

Overview

Net neutrality is both a technical principle and a public policy issue. It impacts competition, free speech, and how businesses and citizens access information.

Key Political Questions

  • Should ISPs be allowed to prioritize some traffic in exchange for payment?
  • Do regulations ensure fair access, or do they risk stifling infrastructure investment?
  • How are marginalized communities affected when ISPs control access?

Arguments for Strong Net Neutrality

  • Protects free expression — equal access to platforms and news sources.
  • Promotes innovation — startups compete on service, not on payments to ISPs.
  • Prevents anti-competitive behavior by large content providers or ISPs.

Arguments Cited Against Strict Regulation

  • ISPs argue that tiered services can fund infrastructure and improve quality for heavy users.
  • Critics say rigid rules may limit flexibility in network management and investment.

Get Involved

Learn about your local representatives, follow technology policy organizations, and read public comments or filings when regulatory actions are proposed. Voting and civic engagement are how policy decisions are shaped.
