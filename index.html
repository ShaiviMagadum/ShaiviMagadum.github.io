What is Net Neutrality?

Net neutrality is the principle that Internet Service Providers (ISPs) must treat all data equally. It means that packets of data—whether video, music, or text—should not be blocked, throttled, or given priority.

Net neutrality protects openness and scalability of the internet, ensuring innovation and free speech.

Unit 1: Data

Data on the internet is made of bits (0s and 1s). A byte is 8 bits. Sometimes roundoff errors occur when precision is lost.

Analog data changes smoothly (like sound). To store it digitally, we sample and encode it.

Compression helps: lossless compression preserves everything, while lossy removes some data.

Metadata is "data about data," like file size or author.

Unit 2: Networks

A computing device connects to a computer network, sending data in packets along a path. Routers pick the fastest route. Protocols like TCP, UDP, and HTTP guide communication.

The internet is fault tolerant and designed with redundancy, so if one path fails, data takes another. Its scalability allows billions of devices.

Understanding Net Neutrality

Net neutrality is a principle that ensures all data on the internet is treated equally. This means that Internet Service Providers (ISPs) cannot discriminate or charge differently based on user, content, website, or platform.

Without net neutrality, ISPs could prioritize traffic from large corporations, slow down services from smaller competitors, or even block access to certain sites. This threatens the open nature of the internet and limits user choice.

The concept gained global attention in the early 2000s as debates over internet regulation intensified. Supporters argue that neutrality keeps the internet fault tolerant, scalable, and fair for innovation, while opponents claim ISPs need flexibility to manage bandwidth.

Net neutrality matters because it ensures equal opportunity for startups, protects free speech, and preserves the internet as a platform for knowledge and democracy.

Political Ramifications

Without net neutrality, ISPs could favor some websites by giving them higher bandwidth or throttling others. This threatens competition and free expression.

Governments debate how protocols and laws should regulate ISPs, balancing business interests with fairness.

Net neutrality policies shape how society accesses information, impacting democracy, economics, and innovation.
