What is Net Neutrality?

Net neutrality is the principle that Internet Service Providers (ISPs) must treat all data on the internet equally. It means that packets of data—whether video, music, or text—should not be blocked, throttled, or given priority based on content, sender, or receiver.

Without net neutrality, ISPs could manipulate bandwidth, charge extra fees, or create unfair advantages. This impacts not only everyday users but also businesses, political campaigns, and freedom of expression online.

Net neutrality protects the openness and scalability of the internet, ensuring that innovation and free speech are preserved.

Unit 1: Data Concepts

  • Bit: The smallest unit of data, either 0 or 1.
  • Byte: A sequence of 8 bits, used to store information.
  • Roundoff: Errors that occur when limited bits can’t perfectly represent a number.
  • Analog Data: Smoothly changing values, like sound or light.
  • Lossless: Compression that preserves all data exactly.
  • Lossy: Compression that discards some data for smaller file sizes.
  • Metadata: Data about data, like a photo’s resolution or author.

Unit 2: Computer Systems & Networks

  • Computing Device: Any device that can run a program.
  • Computer Network: A group of devices that exchange data.
  • Protocol: Rules for how systems communicate.
  • Fault Tolerant: A system that works even when parts fail.
  • Scalability: Ability to grow and handle more demand.
  • Bandwidth: Maximum data transfer rate.
  • Packets: Small pieces of data messages are split into.
  • Routers: Devices that forward packets along the fastest path.
  • DNS: Translates domain names into IP addresses.
  • HTTP: Protocol for accessing the World Wide Web.

Political Ramifications

Net neutrality is also a political issue. Governments and corporations debate whether the internet should be regulated to ensure fairness, or whether ISPs should control traffic. Removing net neutrality could give ISPs power over URLs and web access, impacting democracy, business competition, and innovation.
